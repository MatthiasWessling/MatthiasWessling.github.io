## What this notebook covers
This notebook explores the fundamental and applied aspects of wetting phenomena to enhance the performance and stability of porous media in membrane and electrochemical systems. You will see how we use advanced, non-invasive visualization tools like MRI and FLIM to quantify spatio-temporal wetting patterns and identify active reaction zones at the triple-phase boundary in real-time. Our research introduces high-precision methodologies such as impedance porometry, which measures ion currents to determine pore size distributions with significantly greater accuracy than traditional flow-based methods. We also investigate the complex dynamics of state transitions, such as the row-by-row "zipping" mechanism during the Cassie-Baxter to Wenzel transition and the impact of potential-driven electrowetting on gas diffusion electrodes. Finally, you can expect to find how we engineer surfaces through polyelectrolyte pore bridging or by tailoring zeta potential to stabilize fluid-fluid displacement and control viscous fingering.

## Main insights
- Wetting is a omnipresent phenomenon affecting membrane and porous electrode properties
- Understanding its spatio-temporal properties is the next frontier in electrochemical membrane reactors

# Content
This notebook is a comprehensive collection of scientific research focused on **wetting phenomena, fluid distribution in porous media, and the performance of advanced membranes and gas diffusion electrodes (GDEs)**. The research primarily stems from groups at RWTH Aachen University and the University of Twente, often featuring researchers like Matthias Wessling, Sebastian Brosch, and Anna Maria Kalde.

The core topics covered in this notebook include:

### 1. Membrane Technology and Performance
The sources investigate how the surface properties and wetting behavior of membranes affect their filtration efficiency.
*   **Microsieve Membranes:** Research explores the role of wetting on the clean water flux of silicon nitride microsieves, noting that variations in surface properties (like oxidation or aging) necessitate hydrophilization to maintain stable performance.
*   **Impedance Porometry:** A new method is introduced to determine **pore size distribution** using electrical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) instead of traditional flow measurements, offering higher precision and less sensitivity to pressure measurement errors.
*   **Multibore Membranes:** The notebook includes in-situ investigations using **Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)** to quantify fluid distribution patterns and wetting degrees inside polymeric multibore membranes during dead-end filtration.

### 2. Gas Diffusion Electrodes (GDEs) for $CO_2$ Reduction
A significant portion of the material focuses on electrochemical $CO_2$ reduction (ecCO2RR), a critical technology for mitigating climate change by converting $CO_2$ into valuable chemicals like $CO$.
*   **Triple-Phase Boundary Visualization:** Advanced microscopy techniques are used to visualize exactly where $CO$ is formed within the porous structure, revealing that the highest concentrations occur near the **triple-phase boundary** (where gas, liquid electrolyte, and solid catalyst meet).
*   **Electrowetting and Flooding:** The research examines "electrowetting," where an applied electrical potential alters the wettability of the electrode, often leading to undesirable flooding that can hinder reactant supply and encourage parasitic hydrogen evolution reactions (HER).
*   **Reaction Monitoring:** Using **Fluorescence Lifetime Imaging Microscopy (FLIM)**, researchers track local pH changes in operando to identify active reaction zones and understand how they move as the electrode floods.

### 3. Advanced Modeling and Physics of Wetting
The notebook combines experimental data with numerical simulations to better understand complex transport in microporous networks.
*   **Numerical Simulations:** Sources describe the use of **Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH)** to simulate fluid distribution in mixed-wetting materials, validating these models against microfluidic "micromodels" that mimic real GDE structures.
*   **Superhydrophobicity:** Fundamental physics papers explore the **spontaneous breakdown of superhydrophobicity**, documenting the transition from the Cassie-Baxter state (where liquid sits on air pockets) to the Wenzel state (where liquid fills the surface grooves) through a "zipping" mechanism.
*   **Surface Modification:** The studies frequently employ surface treatments like oxygen plasma, silanization, or polyelectrolyte layer-by-layer coatings to precisely tailor the hydrophilicity, hydrophobicity, or surface charge (zeta potential) of the porous structures.
